Why are scientists studying stem cells?

If someone as severely burned or had a genetic blood condition, imagine if doctors could replace the damaged skin or blood cells with fresh ones. This is the potential of stem cells! Scientists all across the world are researching stem cells to figure out how they work and how they may be utilized to mend bodies and heal diseases.

What's unique about stem cells?

All cells in your body began as stem cells. As a stem cell matures, it differentiates or develops into a specialized cell with a defined purpose. So a stem cell can develop into a red blood cell that transports oxygen, a brain cell that transmits information throughout your body, a skin cell that shields your body, and so on.

An embryonic stem cell is the most useful type of stem cell for scientists' research. This is because this sort of stem cell has the ability to differentiate into a wide variety of cells. However, embryonic stem cells are difficult to obtain.

What are scientists discovering about stem cells?

In 2012, Dr. Shinya Yamanaka and his colleagues made a significant discovery. They discovered how to induce, or turn, a differentiated cell from an adult human back into a stem cell. Dr. Yamanaka dubbed them "induced pluripotent stem cell," or iPS. They then developed this stem cell into a whole other type of differentiated cell! Scientists all throughout the world can now conduct research using these new types of stem cells. They seek to understand more about stem cells and how they may be employed to heal the body and treat diseases.

How do stem cells grow and differentiate—in the human body and in the lab?

Totipotent Stem Cells

Human life begins with a fertilized egg. That egg divides, then divides again, resulting in additional cells. The embryo's cells start off as totipotent stem cells. Some of these cells mature into the placenta, which provides nourishment to the developing embryo. Others become the embryo itself.

Pluripotent Stem Cells

The early embryo is composed of pluripotent stem cells. These cells can differentiate into practically any type of cell!

Multipotent Stem Cells

The growing embryo sends chemical signals instructing its pluripotent stem cells to differentiate into one of three germ layers: ectoderm, mesoderm, or endoderm. These layers consist of multipotent stem cells. They can still differentiate into other types of cells, but only within their germ layer.

Differentiated Cells

As the embryo matures into a fetus and ultimately a baby, the cells in the three germ layers develop into various organs and tissues in the body. A differentiated cell is a specialized cell that performs a specific job, such as a neuron or skin cell (ectoderm layer), lung and pancreatic cells (endoderm layer), and heart muscle and red blood cells (mesoderm layer).

Induced Pluripotent Stem Cells (iPS)

In the lab, scientists can convert regular adult differentiated cells back into a novel type of stem cell. They are known as induced pluripotent stem cells (iPS). Scientists can then use the same procedure to transform an iPS into an entirely different type of cell: the iPS sorts into one of three germ layers before differentiating into a specialized cell within that layer.
